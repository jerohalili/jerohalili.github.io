The problem

Technobyte needed consistent, high-quality social media presence to grow its audience and demonstrate authority in its space. Creating that content was consuming several hours per week — writing posts, sourcing or generating images, scheduling across platforms. The work was important but highly repetitive.

As Marketing Executive, I had direct visibility into this bottleneck. I proposed building a system to automate it entirely.

The solution

An end-to-end automation pipeline that runs continuously without human input, producing on-brand content at a fraction of the previous cost.

Architecture

  • n8n — Workflow orchestration, scheduling, and inter-service routing
  • LLaMA 3 (via Ollama) — Local LLM for text generation, running on owned hardware
  • ComfyUI — Local image generation pipeline with consistent style settings
  • Social scheduling API — Final publishing layer
  • Telegram bot — Human review touchpoint (one-tap approve / regenerate)

Flow

At scheduled intervals, n8n triggers the pipeline. The LLM receives a structured prompt containing brand voice guidelines and the content category for that slot. It returns a draft post and a set of image keywords. Those keywords feed into ComfyUI, which generates a matching image. Both assets route to a lightweight review step before being pushed to the scheduler.

The review step exists to catch outliers, not to rewrite everything. Well-tuned prompts keep the human intervention rate below 5% of generated posts.

My role

I designed and built the full system as the sole engineer. Separately, as Marketing Executive, I defined the content strategy, brand voice guidelines, and performance metrics — which directly informed the prompt architecture and scheduling cadence.

This dual role was unusual but powerful: the person engineering the system was the same person who deeply understood what it needed to produce.

Results

The system ran for months with minimal intervention. Posting frequency tripled. Content quality remained consistent — in some cases improving because the scheduling was now optimized rather than whenever someone had time. Reach increased significantly as consistency compounded over time.

Total marginal cost per piece of content: zero. The hardware was already owned. The software was open source. The only ongoing cost was electricity.

What I learned

Automation succeeds when the prompt is a first-class artifact — versioned, tested, and refined like code. The difference between mediocre and excellent automated content is almost entirely in the quality of the prompt engineering, not the model size.
